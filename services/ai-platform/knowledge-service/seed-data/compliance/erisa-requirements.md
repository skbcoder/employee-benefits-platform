# ERISA Compliance Requirements

## Overview

The Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974 (ERISA) sets minimum standards for most voluntarily established health and welfare benefit plans in private industry. ERISA provides protections for plan participants and beneficiaries and establishes standards of conduct for plan fiduciaries.

## Plan Document Requirements

ERISA requires that every employee benefit plan be established and maintained pursuant to a written plan document that includes:

- A named fiduciary with authority to control and manage plan operations
- A procedure for establishing and carrying out a funding policy
- A description of benefits and the conditions for receiving them
- A procedure for amending the plan and the basis for plan termination
- A claims procedure including appeal rights

## Summary Plan Description (SPD)

The SPD is the primary document through which participants learn about their benefits. ERISA requires the SPD to include:

- Plan name and type
- Plan sponsor name, address, and EIN
- Plan administrator name, address, and phone number
- Agent for service of legal process
- Description of benefits and eligibility requirements
- Circumstances that may result in disqualification, ineligibility, or denial of benefits
- Sources of plan financing
- Procedures for filing claims and appealing denied claims
- Statement of ERISA rights

The SPD must be provided to new participants within 90 days of becoming covered and updated every 5 years if there are plan amendments, or every 10 years regardless.

## Fiduciary Responsibilities

Plan fiduciaries must:

- Act solely in the interest of plan participants and beneficiaries
- Act for the exclusive purpose of providing benefits and defraying reasonable plan expenses
- Act with the care, skill, prudence, and diligence of a prudent person
- Diversify plan investments to minimize the risk of large losses (for funded plans)
- Act in accordance with the plan documents insofar as they are consistent with ERISA

Fiduciaries who breach their duties may be personally liable for losses to the plan and may be subject to other equitable and remedial relief.

## Claims and Appeals Procedures

### Initial Claims
- Claims must be filed according to the plan's reasonable claims procedures
- The plan must respond to claims within specific timeframes:
  - Urgent care claims: 72 hours
  - Pre-service claims: 15 days (may extend 15 days)
  - Post-service claims: 30 days (may extend 15 days)
  - Disability claims: 45 days (may extend 30 days twice)

### Denial Notices
If a claim is denied, the plan must provide written notice including:
- Specific reasons for the denial
- Reference to the plan provisions on which the denial is based
- Description of additional information needed (if any)
- Description of the plan's appeal procedures and applicable time limits
- Statement of the right to bring a civil action under ERISA Section 502(a) after exhausting appeals

### Internal Appeals
- Participants have at least 180 days to file an appeal
- The appeal must be reviewed by someone other than the person who made the initial denial
- The reviewer must give no deference to the initial adverse determination
- For medical claims, the reviewer must consult with a healthcare professional with appropriate expertise
- Appeal decisions must be provided within:
  - Urgent care: 72 hours
  - Pre-service: 30 days
  - Post-service: 60 days

### External Review
After exhausting internal appeals, participants may request an independent external review. External review is conducted by an independent review organization (IRO) and results in a binding decision.

## COBRA Administration Under ERISA

ERISA-covered plans must comply with COBRA requirements:
- Provide initial COBRA notice to employees and spouses within 90 days of coverage
- Provide qualifying event notice within 14 days of learning of the event
- Maintain records of all COBRA notices and elections

## Annual Reporting (Form 5500)

ERISA requires the filing of Form 5500 (Annual Return/Report) with the Department of Labor:
- Due by the last day of the seventh month after the plan year ends (July 31 for calendar year plans)
- Extension available by filing Form 5558 (2.5 months)
- Large plans (100+ participants) must include an independent auditor's report
- Schedule A (insurance information) and other applicable schedules must be attached

## Prohibited Transactions

ERISA prohibits certain transactions between the plan and "parties in interest" (fiduciaries, service providers, plan sponsors, etc.):
- Sale, exchange, or lease of property between plan and party in interest
- Lending money or extending credit between plan and party in interest
- Furnishing goods, services, or facilities between plan and party in interest
- Transfer or use of plan assets to or by a party in interest

Certain exemptions exist for reasonable service arrangements and adequate compensation.

## Preemption

ERISA preempts (supersedes) state laws that relate to employee benefit plans. This means that state insurance mandates, state causes of action, and other state laws generally do not apply to ERISA-covered self-insured health plans. However, ERISA does not preempt state insurance laws that regulate insured plans.

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

| Violation | Penalty |
|-----------|---------|
| Failure to provide SPD | Up to $110 per day per participant |
| Failure to file Form 5500 | Up to $250 per day, max $150,000 |
| Failure to provide required COBRA notices | $110 per day per affected beneficiary |
| Breach of fiduciary duty | Personal liability for losses, equitable relief |
| Prohibited transactions | 15% excise tax (100% if not corrected) |

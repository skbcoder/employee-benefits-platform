# HIPAA Privacy and Security Compliance

## Overview

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996 establishes national standards for the protection of individually identifiable health information. As a plan sponsor, the company must comply with HIPAA's Privacy Rule, Security Rule, and Breach Notification Rule.

## Protected Health Information (PHI)

Protected Health Information includes any individually identifiable health information held or transmitted by a covered entity or business associate. PHI includes:

- Employee names linked to health plan enrollment
- Dates of service, birth dates, admission and discharge dates
- Social Security numbers, health plan beneficiary numbers
- Medical diagnoses, treatment records, prescription information
- Biometric identifiers, photographs
- Any other information that could identify a specific individual in connection with their health status

## Minimum Necessary Standard

When using or disclosing PHI, the company must make reasonable efforts to limit access to the minimum necessary information needed to accomplish the intended purpose. This applies to:

- Internal uses of PHI for plan administration
- Disclosures to business associates, providers, and other covered entities
- Requests for PHI from other covered entities

The minimum necessary standard does not apply to disclosures to the individual, disclosures authorized by the individual, or disclosures required by law.

## Employee Rights Under HIPAA

### Right to Access
Employees have the right to inspect and obtain copies of their PHI held by the health plan within 30 days of a written request. A reasonable, cost-based fee may be charged for copies.

### Right to Amend
Employees may request amendments to their PHI if they believe the information is incorrect or incomplete. The plan must respond within 60 days.

### Right to an Accounting of Disclosures
Employees may request a list of disclosures of their PHI made by the plan for purposes other than treatment, payment, or healthcare operations during the prior 6 years.

### Right to Request Restrictions
Employees may request restrictions on the use and disclosure of their PHI. The plan is not required to agree to restrictions but must honor any agreed-upon restrictions.

### Right to Request Confidential Communications
Employees may request that the plan communicate PHI through alternative means or at alternative locations (e.g., sending correspondence to a P.O. box instead of a home address).

### Right to File a Complaint
Employees may file a complaint with the plan's Privacy Officer or the Department of Health and Human Services (HHS) Office for Civil Rights if they believe their privacy rights have been violated.

## Security Rule Requirements

The HIPAA Security Rule requires administrative, physical, and technical safeguards to protect electronic PHI (ePHI):

### Administrative Safeguards
- Designated Security Officer responsible for ePHI security
- Workforce security training upon hire and annually
- Information access management and role-based access controls
- Security incident procedures and response plans
- Periodic security risk assessments (at least annually)
- Business associate agreements for all vendors handling ePHI

### Physical Safeguards
- Facility access controls (badge access, visitor logs)
- Workstation security (screen locks, privacy screens)
- Device and media controls (encrypted laptops, secure disposal of storage media)
- Server room access restricted to authorized personnel

### Technical Safeguards
- Access controls (unique user IDs, emergency access procedures)
- Audit controls (system activity logs, login monitoring)
- Integrity controls (mechanisms to verify ePHI has not been improperly altered)
- Transmission security (encryption for ePHI transmitted over networks)
- Automatic logoff after 15 minutes of inactivity

## Breach Notification Requirements

### What Constitutes a Breach
A breach is an impermissible use or disclosure of PHI that compromises the security or privacy of the information. A risk assessment must be performed to determine if the incident constitutes a reportable breach considering:
- Nature and extent of the PHI involved
- The unauthorized person who used or received the PHI
- Whether the PHI was actually acquired or viewed
- Extent to which the risk has been mitigated

### Notification Timeline
- Individual notification: Within 60 days of discovering the breach
- HHS notification: Within 60 days for breaches affecting 500+ individuals; annually for breaches affecting fewer than 500 individuals
- Media notification: Within 60 days for breaches affecting 500+ individuals in a state or jurisdiction

### Notification Content
Breach notifications must include:
- Description of the breach and the PHI involved
- Steps the individual should take to protect themselves
- Description of what the organization is doing to investigate, mitigate, and prevent future breaches
- Contact information for the organization's Privacy Officer

## Plan Administration and the Firewall

The company, as plan sponsor, has established a "firewall" between the group health plan and the employer's other functions. Only authorized individuals in HR Benefits and designated plan administrators may access PHI for plan administration purposes. PHI may not be used for employment decisions, marketing, or any purpose unrelated to plan administration.

## Penalties for HIPAA Violations

| Tier | Violation Type | Penalty per Violation | Annual Maximum |
|------|---------------|----------------------|----------------|
| 1 | Unknowing violation | $100 - $50,000 | $25,000 |
| 2 | Reasonable cause, not willful neglect | $1,000 - $50,000 | $100,000 |
| 3 | Willful neglect, corrected within 30 days | $10,000 - $50,000 | $250,000 |
| 4 | Willful neglect, not corrected | $50,000 | $1,500,000 |

Criminal penalties may also apply for knowing violations, including fines up to $250,000 and imprisonment up to 10 years.

## Business Associate Agreements

Any vendor, contractor, or service provider that creates, receives, maintains, or transmits PHI on behalf of the plan must sign a Business Associate Agreement (BAA) before accessing PHI. BAAs must include:
- Permitted uses and disclosures of PHI
- Requirement to implement appropriate safeguards
- Requirement to report breaches and security incidents
- Return or destruction of PHI upon termination
- Compliance with HHS audit requirements

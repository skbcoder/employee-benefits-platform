# Section 125 Cafeteria Plan

## Overview

The company maintains a Section 125 Cafeteria Plan under the Internal Revenue Code, allowing eligible employees to pay for certain benefits on a pre-tax basis. This reduces taxable income for federal income tax, Social Security tax (FICA), and in most states, state income tax.

## Eligible Benefits Under the Cafeteria Plan

The following benefits may be elected on a pre-tax basis through the cafeteria plan:
- Medical insurance premiums (employee share)
- Dental insurance premiums
- Vision insurance premiums
- Health Savings Account (HSA) contributions
- Flexible Spending Account (FSA) contributions:
  - Healthcare FSA
  - Dependent Care FSA

## Pre-Tax vs. Post-Tax Elections

### Pre-Tax (Default for Eligible Benefits)
Employee premium contributions are deducted from gross pay before federal and state income taxes and FICA taxes are calculated. This reduces the employee's taxable income.

Example: An employee earning $60,000/year with $3,600 in annual medical premiums on a pre-tax basis would have a taxable income of $56,400 for income tax purposes.

### Post-Tax
Some supplemental benefits are paid on a post-tax basis:
- Supplemental life insurance (employee-paid portion)
- Supplemental AD&D insurance
- Long-term disability (post-tax election allows tax-free benefit payments)

## Mid-Year Election Changes

Section 125 requires that elections remain in effect for the entire plan year unless the employee experiences a qualifying change in status event (see Qualifying Life Events policy). This IRS rule exists because pre-tax treatment requires a binding election.

Permitted mid-year changes must be consistent with the life event:
- Birth of a child → may add the child and increase coverage
- Marriage → may add spouse and change tier
- Loss of spouse's coverage → may enroll or upgrade

Changes not consistent with the life event are not permitted. For example, gaining a dependent does not allow removing dental coverage.

## Healthcare Flexible Spending Account (Healthcare FSA)

### Contribution Limits
- Maximum annual contribution: $3,200 (2024 limit, indexed annually)
- Minimum annual contribution: $100

### Eligible Expenses
Healthcare FSA funds may be used for qualified medical expenses not covered by insurance:
- Deductibles, copays, and coinsurance
- Prescription medications
- Dental and orthodontic expenses not covered by dental plan
- Vision expenses (glasses, contacts, LASIK)
- Hearing aids and related expenses
- Mental health counseling copays

### Use-It-or-Lose-It Rule
Healthcare FSA funds must be used by the end of the plan year. The company provides a $640 carryover provision, allowing unused funds up to $640 to roll over to the next plan year. Amounts exceeding $640 are forfeited.

### Claims Submission
Employees must submit claims with receipts within 90 days after the end of the plan year. Claims may be submitted electronically through the FSA administrator's portal or mobile app.

## Dependent Care Flexible Spending Account (DCFSA)

### Contribution Limits
- Maximum annual contribution: $5,000 ($2,500 if married filing separately)
- Both spouses may not exceed $5,000 combined if married

### Eligible Expenses
DCFSA funds may be used for dependent care expenses that enable the employee (and spouse, if applicable) to work:
- Daycare and preschool for children under 13
- Before and after school programs for children under 13
- Summer day camp for children under 13
- Elder care for dependents who live with the employee
- Au pair or nanny expenses (for care, not household duties)

### Ineligible Expenses
- Overnight camps
- Tutoring or educational expenses
- Food, clothing, or entertainment
- Healthcare expenses for dependents

### Tax Interaction
The DCFSA benefit is coordinated with the Dependent Care Tax Credit (IRS Form 2441). Employees should consider which option provides greater tax savings based on their income level and filing status.

## HSA Interaction

Employees enrolled in an HSA-eligible high-deductible health plan (HDHP) may contribute to a Health Savings Account through the cafeteria plan on a pre-tax basis. HSA contribution limits for the current year:
- Individual coverage: $4,150
- Family coverage: $8,300
- Catch-up contribution (age 55+): Additional $1,000

HSA contributions through the cafeteria plan are subject to the same mid-year change restrictions as other Section 125 elections, except that HSA contribution amounts may be changed at any time during the plan year (IRS Revenue Ruling 2002-27).

## Nondiscrimination Testing

Section 125 plans must satisfy three nondiscrimination tests:
1. **Eligibility Test**: The plan must not discriminate in favor of highly compensated individuals as to eligibility to participate
2. **Benefits and Contributions Test**: The plan must not discriminate in favor of highly compensated participants as to contributions and benefits
3. **Key Employee Concentration Test**: No more than 25% of the aggregate benefits may be provided to key employees

Testing is performed annually. If the plan fails, excess benefits for highly compensated individuals may be included in their gross income.
